Wikipedia Mobile Apps — Reader Experience

The Wikipedia Mobile Visual Article Summary

A feature concept by Raoul Kahn — built for the Senior Product Manager, Mobile Apps role at the Wikimedia Foundation.

The context

The Wikipedia Mobile Apps role is focused on delivering the reader strategy — with a specific emphasis on improving the experience for the most committed app users. In March 2026, Wikimedia published their reader strategy, noting that pageviews have been declining as readers turn to AI and social media for information. The goal: make Wikipedia immediately useful, memorable, and enjoyable so readers return, go deeper, and eventually contribute.

This is one idea worth testing. Wikipedia articles like Apple Inc. are extraordinary — but on mobile, decades of dense information can feel like a wall. I wanted to explore whether a simple, optional visual entry point could make that depth more approachable without changing anything about the article itself.

Apple Inc. is an American multinational technology company headquartered in Cupertino, California, in Silicon Valley, best known for its consumer electronics, software and online services. Founded in 1976 as Apple Computer Company by Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak and Ronald Wayne, the company was incorporated by Jobs and Wozniak as Apple Computer, Inc. the following year.

It was renamed to its current name in 2007 as the company had expanded its focus from computers to consumer electronics. Apple is one of the Big Tech companies.

History

1976–1980: Founding and incorporation

In 1976, Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak co-founded Apple in Jobs' parents' home on Crist Drive in Los Altos, California. Wozniak called the popular belief that the company was founded in the garage "a bit of a myth", although they moved some operations to the garage when the bedroom became too crowded.

Apple Computer Company was founded on April 1, 1976, by Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and Ronald Wayne as a partnership. The company's first product was the Apple I, a computer designed and hand-built entirely by Wozniak. To finance its creation, Jobs sold his Volkswagen Bus, and Wozniak sold his HP-65 calculator.

The Apple II, also designed by Wozniak, was introduced on April 16, 1977. It differed from its major rivals, the TRS-80 and Commodore PET, because of its character cell-based color graphics and open architecture.

On December 12, 1980, Apple went public with an initial public offering (IPO) on the fully electronic Nasdaq stock market, selling 4.6 million shares at $22 per share, generating over $100 million — more capital than any IPO since Ford Motor Company in 1956.

1980–1990: Success with Macintosh

In November and December 1979, Steve Jobs and Apple employees visited Xerox PARC, where they observed the Xerox Alto, featuring a graphical user interface (GUI) and a mouse. This visit influenced Jobs to implement a GUI in Apple's products starting with the Apple Lisa in 1983.

In 1984, Apple launched the Macintosh, the first personal computer without a bundled programming language. Its debut was signified by "1984", a US$1.5-million television advertisement directed by Ridley Scott that aired during Super Bowl XVIII.

1990–1997: Decline and restructuring

Throughout this period, Microsoft continued to gain market share with Windows by focusing on delivering software to inexpensive personal computers, while Apple was delivering a richly engineered but expensive experience.

With Apple only weeks away from bankruptcy, the board preferred NeXTSTEP and purchased NeXT in late 1996 for $400 million, retaining Steve Jobs.

1997–2007: Return to profitability

Jobs canceled 70% of models, ending 3,000 jobs and paring to the core of its computer offerings. The next month, Steve Jobs convinced Microsoft to make a $150-million investment in Apple.

On May 6, 1998, Apple introduced a new all-in-one computer reminiscent of the original Macintosh: the iMac. The iMac sold 800,000 units in its first five months.

In 2003, the iTunes Store was introduced with music downloads for 99¢ a song and iPod integration. It quickly became the market leader in online music services, with over 5 billion downloads by June 19, 2008.

2007–2011: Success with mobile devices

During his keynote speech at the Macworld Expo on January 9, 2007, Jobs announced the renaming of Apple Computer, Inc. to Apple Inc., because the company had broadened its focus from computers to consumer electronics. This event also saw the announcement of the iPhone and the Apple TV.

In July 2008, Apple launched the App Store to sell third-party applications for the iPhone and iPod Touch. Within a month, the store sold 60 million applications.

The Hypothesis

Wikimedia's own carousel experiments showed 8.7% engagement — well above typical benchmarks — and increased next-day return visits. That was an image gallery. This extends the concept further: a narrative summary carousel that helps committed readers understand an article's arc before deciding to read it in full. Different purpose, same validated mechanic.

📊

The Problem

Committed app readers drop off when confronted with dense, unstructured long-form articles on mobile. The content is extraordinary — but the entry point is a wall of text.

💡

The Idea

A single optional button near the top of long articles unlocks a swipeable narrative summary — curated from existing article content, chapter by chapter. No AI-generated content. No changes to the article. Just a better on-ramp. Designed as optional to preserve the core reading experience.

📈

Expected Impact

Button tap rate. Session depth and time in app. 7-day return visit rate. "Read full article" conversion from the final slide. Could later guide users toward lightweight contributions.

↑ Session depth · ↑ Return visits · ↑ Article completion · ↑ Contributor readiness
🛡️

Design Principles

Summaries are derived entirely from existing article content — preserving accuracy and neutrality. MVP targets high-traffic articles first to validate before scaling.
